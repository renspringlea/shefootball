*My [recent analysis]({% post_url 2026-06-20-defensive-corners %}) examined our defensive corners. This article extends my analysis to attacking corners.*  

A caution: I'm not personally involved in attacking corners like I am with defensive corners. I'm less familiar with strategy, and I'm not comfortable with critiquing others' play like I am with critiquing my own play. But since Watties asked me to analyse attacking corners, I'll do my best to extract useful insights from the data!  

Over eight matches, we attacked 43 corners. This makes an average of 5.4 corners attacked per match (minimum of 2 in one match, and a maximum of 12). Notably, this is below both a) the average corners-per-game from published studies¹ and b) the number of corners we defended during those same eight games (59 corners, or 7.4 per match).  

Crucially, we're solid at attacking corners — we scored 4 goals from 43 corners (above average). All corner balls made it into the 18-yard box, and there is a decent spread in the areas where the ball is first landing.  

I've identified 3 areas where we can take further advantage of our attacking corners:  
1. Ensuring that, after the ball is taken, we get the first touch (rather than letting our opponents get a touch or letting the ball exit the 18-yard box without any contact).  
2. Having a midfielder sit in the D (just outside the 18-yard box) ready to recycle the ball back into another attack.  
3. Delivering the ball a bit higher up the field - between the penalty spot and the 6-yard box, rather than inside the 6-yard box). This point is up to Watties though, because there is room for strategic disagreement here.  

## 1. Corner balls' first contact    

First, I wanted to see how where our corner takers are actually delivering the ball.  

I used the same notation system from my [recent analysis on defensive corners]({% post_url 2026-06-20-defensive-corners %}), in which the pitch is divided into 16 rectangular zones. To account for the fact that corners are taken from both sides, all corners taken from the left-hand side were recorded as-is, but all corners taken from the right-hand side were inverted. This means that the diagram shows the near post on the *left* of your screen and the far post on the *right* of your screen, which might not necessarily match the actual direction in which the kick was taken. In other words, the corner taker is always standing at the bottom-left of the diagram and kicking across your screen from left to right.  

The following diagram shows the 16 zones, labelled according to the percent of defensive corners in which the ball first touched either the ground or a player. The zones are also coloured, with darker rectangles corresponding to more balls landing there.  

<a target="_blank" href="https://she.football/assets/images/3_heatmap.png"><img alt="Heatmap of where corner balls are landing" src="https://she.football/assets/images/3_heatmap.png" /></a>    

All corner balls landed somewhere inside the 18-yard box, except for the 2 corners (4.7%) that went directly out of play. No corner balls failed to make it into the 18-yard box (a fairly common weakness that our opponents had in the defensive corners analysis).  

The most common zones into which corners landed were:    
- 14 corners (32.6%) — Zone 1B, inside the goal area on the near half  
- 7 corners (16.3%) — Zone 2B, in the strip between the goal area and the penalty spot on the near half  

All other corners were fairly evenly distributed in the near three-quarters of the 18-yard box. No corners made it into the quarter of the 18-yard box furthest from the corner taker.  

## 2. Outcomes of corners  

Next, I wanted to know what happens after we kick a corner. Are we successful or unsuccessful at attacking corners?  

To record outcomes, I watched all 43 attacking corners and assigned them to the same outcome categories as in my defensive analysis — e.g., ball cleared out of the 18 yard box, goal, attempt off target (missed shot), and so on.  

The following column graph shows the 43 attacking corners categorised into those various outcomes:  

<a target="_blank" href="https://she.football/assets/images/3_outcome.png"><img alt="Column graph of corner outcomes" src="https://she.football/assets/images/3_outcome.png" /></a>  

The clearest finding from that column graph is that we seem to be successful at attacking corners.  

Across 43 corners, we scored 4 goals (9.3%)! This is above the average rate of ~3% from published studies¹. A further 5 corners resulted in a shot that either missed the goal or was blocked by a defender. That makes a total of 20.9% of corners that we are converting into either a shot or a goal — not bad.  

What characteristics distinguish the 4 corners that resulted in a goal? I noticed two patterns. We should proceed with caution here, because 4 goals is a small sample size — so these patterns should be interpreted as suggestive, not conclusive.  

The two characteristics of goal-scoring corners were:  
1. Zone of delivery. Three out of four goal-scoring corners were delivered *outside the six-yard box* — see the labelled diagram below. This means that the corners resulting in goals were also corners that were delivered further up the pitch than our typical corner deliveries.  
2. First touch. Four out of four goal-scoring were touched first by a SMT player, not an opponent.

## 3. Potential strategic adjustments  

The above evidence points to some strategic adjustments that we can make so that we're taking full advantage of our attacking corners.  

As I mentioned above, this is a decision for the attacking players and Watties to make — I don't play on that end of the pitch, and (Watties' dream of me scoring a hat-trick notwithstanding), I definitely don't know as much about scoring goals as I do about saving them. There are also many considerations that aren't present in my data — so the following information should be interpreted as ideas to consider, not prescriptions.  

Firstly, for players waiting to receive the ball in the box — are there any ways we can ensure that we get the first touch, rather than letting our opponents get a touch or letting the ball exit the 18-yard box? All four goals were scored after we got the first touch. For example, this could involve moving more before the corner is taken, developing our skills in losing defenders/man-markers, etc.  

Secondly, we might want to have a midfielder sit in the D (just outside the 18-yard box) ready to recycle the ball back into another attack.  
- In the corners were the ball exited the 18 yard box without any touches (3 corners, 5.2%), the reason was invariably that we had no backup player sitting around the edge of the 18-yard box ready to receive the ball.  
- This was also common in the corners that the opposition successfully cleared — many of these clearances were actually fairly panicked boots, and having a player ready to intercept these balls and recycle them back into the 18-yard box would give us more opportunities for shots on goal.  

Thirdly, for corner takers — we might want to adjust where the corner balls are being delivered.  

This is my spiciest take! And I'm not 100% confident that this is the right choice — there is room for strategic disagreement here. But I think it's worth experimenting with.  

To elaborate: as a goalkeeper, I often face balls delivered into the 6-yard box. But I can reach those balls — anything inside the 6-yard box is where goalkeepers can attempt to catch, punch, or block the ball. The balls that scare me more are the ones delivered into the "second six", which is the space between the 6-yard box and the penalty spot — a bit further away from me. It's still close enough that it's easy to score a goal, but it's too far away for me to directly intercept. The "second six" is what my nightmares are made of.  

My fear of the second six is tentatively supported by published studies, which report higher goal-scoring rates from balls delivered into the second six than into the 6-yard box.  

A peer-reviewed review study reports a result found across several large-scale football datasets¹:  

> [I]t appears that most corners are executed in the center of the box, 6–9 yards from goal, and this zone had the best success rates for goals...  

That study also cautions that the success rate might vary between inswing and outswing kicks.  

Another study, not peer reviewed but clearly written by somebody as nerdy as me, found:  

> The delivery zone that was most likely to result in both shots on-target and goals was GF2...  

("GF2" is that analyst's label for the central region of the second six, i.e., between the penalty spot and the 6-yard box.)  

Returning to our own games, only 15 of our 43 corners (34.9%) were delivered into the second six, but 2 of those 15 resulted in a goal. The sample size is small, and this is a suggestive trend rather than conclusive evidence. Coaches have all sorts of reasons why they might want players to aim for the 6-yard box, or the near post, or the back post.  

But, this could be something for us to try — experimenting with delivering balls a bit higher, to the strip of grass between the 6-yard box and the penalty spot. If I were the goalkeeper and were trying to defend a corner like that, it'd drive me nuts.  

(I'm mindful that this is a longer distance to kick! For our corner takers who are still building strength and confidence, this could be achieved via a short corner. If a player is positioned at the edge of the penalty box, then the corner taker could make a short pass to that edge-of-the-box player, who then taps the ball into the danger zone. Again, up to Watties and the attacking players, but this could be something to experiment with.)
