I watched our eight recorded matches to date, and I recorded systematic data about every corner that we defended during these matches.  

Over eight matches, we defended 59 corners. This makes an average of 7.4 corners defended per match (minimum of 0 in one match, and a maximum of 14 in one match).  

Summary if you can't be bothered reading:  
- We’re solid at defending corners, and most corners end in a clearance (only 1 goal conceded from 59 corners)  
- Most corners land around the near post  
- The two defenders stationed at the front and back post directly saved 4 goals over 59 corners (and indirectly contributed to a further 3 clearances)  
- We should probably retain the front- and back-post defenders, as well as a player on the edge of the penalty box closest to the corner taker, as long as we don’t need those 3 players for man-marking  
- I can probably be catching/punching many more corner balls than I currently am  

## 1. Corner balls' first contact  

First, I wanted to see how where opposition players are actually kicking the ball off of a corner.  

To make this systematic, I used a notation system that divides the pitch into 16 rectangular zones. To account for the fact that corners are taken from both sides, all corners taken from the left-hand side were recorded as-is, but all corners taken from the right-hand side were inverted. This means that the diagram shows the near post on the *left* of your screen and the far post on the *right* of your screen, which might not necessarily match the actual direction in which the kick was taken. In other words, the corner taker is always standing at the bottom-left of the diagram and kicking across your screen from left to right.  

<a target="_blank" href="https://she.football/assets/images/2_gridonly.png"><img alt="Diagram of 16-grid system used in this analysis" src="https://she.football/assets/images/2_gridonly.png" /></a>  

For each corner, I recorded which zone in which the ball first touched either the ground or a player. This corresponds to the position on the pitch where the ball is landing or making first contact with either a player (from either team) or the ground — meaning if you stand in that spot, you can challenge for the ball.  

The following diagram shows the same grid of zones from above, but the 16 zones are labelled according to the percent of defensive corners in which the ball first touched either the ground or a player. The zones are also coloured, with darker rectangles corresponding to more balls landing there.  

<a target="_blank" href="https://she.football/assets/images/2_heatmap.png"><img alt="Heatmap of where corner balls are landing" src="https://she.football/assets/images/2_heatmap.png" /></a>  

The most common zones into which corners landed were:  
- 12 corners (20.3%) — Zone 1B, inside the goal area on the near half  
- 11 corners (18.6%) — Zone 2B, in the strip between the goal area and the penalty spot on the near half  
- 11 corners (18.6%) — Zone 6A, completely outside the penalty box on the near side  
- 9 corners (15.3%) — Zone 1A, between the goal area and the edge of the penalty box on the near side  

Notably, corners never landed in:  
- The strip between the penalty spot and the high edge of the penalty box (Zones 3A–3D)  
- Beyond the far edge of the goal area (Zones 1D–3D or 6B)  

There are some useful conclusions here.  

Firstly, there is a pretty narrow range of areas where the corners are landing — around the near post. We might want to simply stack a bunch of players around the near post, since most corners are landing on that side of the pitch.  

Secondly, the most frequent outcome is that the corner lands inside the goal area. Goalkeepers are often advised that they should aim to catch/punch any corner that strays into the goal area. So, I can probably be catching/punching many more balls than I currently am (see below) — if anything, I'm leaving the ball more frequently than I should be.  

Thirdly, corners are frequently non-threatening, at least directly — many corners are either kicked directly out of play (4 corners, 6.8%) or don’t make it into the penalty area (12 corners, 20.3%).  

A caution: this is where the ball first lands. Most corners ended up with some uncontrolled ping pong in the penalty box. So, the zones below that have a “0” are still important to defend, at least once the ball enters play.  

## 2. Outcomes of corners  

Next, I wanted to know what happens after we defend a corner. Are we successful or unsuccessful at defending corners?  

To record outcomes, I watched all 59 corners and assigned them to one of about a dozen outcomes — e.g., ball cleared out of the 18 yard box, goal, attempt off target (missed shot), and so on.  

The following column graph shows the 59 corners categorised into those various outcomes:  

<a target="_blank" href="https://she.football/assets/images/2_outcome.png"><img alt="Column graph of corner outcomes" src="https://she.football/assets/images/2_outcome.png" /></a>  

The clearest finding from that column graph is that we seem to be successful at defending corners.  

Across all 59 corners, we only conceded 1 goal (1.7%). For another 3 corners (5.2%), the attacking team made a shot at goal, but the shot was off-target. In all other cases, either the defenders or the goalkeeper successfully dealt with the ball.  

As mentioned above, the percent for "goalkeeper save/catch/punch" is maybe a bit low — I only personally dealt with 13.8% of corners (8 corners). This is much lower than the 25.6% of corners (15 corners) that are landing in the goal area, which I really should be aiming to catch/punch. This suggests that as a goalkeeper, I should probably work to improve my confidence with dealing with corners myself — this would help us improve our corner defense even more.  

## 3. The ideal setup for corners  

Currently, I usually ask the defenders to arrange themselves in the following setup. In order of priority:  
1. All opposition players in and around the penalty box are marked by one of our players.  
2. We position one player on each of the the front post (zone 1B) and the back post (zone 1C), ready to deflect any shots or rebounds from going into the goal.  
3. One player sitting at the edge of the penalty box nearest to the striker (zone 6A), ready to intercept the ball if it comes to them.  

This is a combination of zone-marking and man-marking. In pre-season, I  based this setup on several published studies of professional football, which showed that the above approach was the most successful strategy for defending corners.  

But does that setup still apply to our team? Now that we have data on our own matches, we can answer this question.  

Regarding the front- and back-post players: either the front-post or the back-post player touched the ball in 7 corners (11.9%). For 4 (6.8%) of those 7 corners, the player actually made a save, i.e. the ball would’ve gone into the goal if not for that player. This means that the front- and back-post players don't often touch the ball, but when they do, it is usually directly preventing a goal. In fact, we would have conceded **4 more goals** this season if we didn't have front- and back-post players. Since many of our games have been won by a 1-goal margin, this is an important edge for us to maintain.  

Regarding the player at the edge of the penalty box: we actually haven't been using this player super often, but I think we should be. The ball went into zone 6A (outside the penalty box) 18.6% of the time — in these cases, the player can directly intercept or challenge for that ball. Furthermore, the ball was just inside the penalty box 22.1% of the time — in these cases, the player can help support the defenders who are positioned there by providing a passing option.  

So, this evidence suggests that we should indeed **keep** our system of:  
- All opponents in or near the box are man-marked  
- Front post, back post  
- Player at the edge of the box  


### Notes on methodology - for nerds only  
- The sample of eight matches corresponds to our nine regular season matches to date, minus the R3 away vs Flinders (2026-04-12) which I couldn’t see on Veo. We also didn’t have footage for the cup match at home vs Jaguars. We did have footage for the preseason friendly away vs Sturt Lions, but this match is highly non-representative so I didn’t include it.  
- The 16-zone notation system is taken from [Tütüncü et al (2024)](https://doi.org/10.1177/17479541241256223).  
- The 14 outcome categories were taken from [Pulling et al (2017)’s study of the EPL](https://doi.org/10.1080/24748668.2013.11868637). I grouped some of these categories together (e.g., goalkeeper save + catch + punch) for the bar graph.
